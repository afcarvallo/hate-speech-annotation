Welcome to the Hate Speech Annotation Guide


Introduction

Hate speech is any form of expression that incites hatred or violence against a group based on characteristics like ethnicity, disability , religion, gender, working class , idelogical group , or sexual orientation.

It can be explicit (direct insults or threats) or implicit (coded language or stereotypes).

This project aims to create the first token-level annotated corpus identifying instances of hate speech focused on specific groups.

The dataset generated from this guide will be used for enhancing Named Entity Recognition (NER) models for detecting hate speech, extending beyond traditional detection methods annotating group mentions , offensive denominations, offensive words and hate speech intent.

This token-level annotation approach provides deeper insights into the context and specific targets of hate speech.

Finally, the dataset and the resulting NER model will increase explainability, helping users understand the detailed decision-making process behind content moderation.


Annotation Process Overview

  • LLM Pre-annotation: Data is pre-processed using a Language Model (LLM) to highlight potential hate speech in a text.
  • Data curation: Your role will consist of reviewing text span annotations made by the LLM.

    • Named Entity Recognition Task Description

    This tasks consists on tagging different elements of text, such as group mentions, offensive denomination , offensive words, and hate speech intent.

    The fundamental premise for all annotation rules in is to annotate the shortest and most general expression possible, ensuring that the entity is fully described. In this sense, modifying words should be excluded.


    This guide helps annotators identify and annotate explicit and implicit forms of hate speech, focusing on:

    • General Rules: Positive and negative rules for all mention labels.
    • Group Mentions: Identify mentions of specific groups (e.g., races, ethnicities, religions).
    • Offensive Denominations: Annotate slurs, derogatory terms, or offensive names for individuals or groups.
    • Hate Speech Intent: Evaluate the intent behind statements, including derogation, threats , hatecrime, comparison and animosity.
    • Offensive Words: Tag offensive words, including insults, threats, or calls for violence.

    General Rules

    Positive, negative, and multi-word rules that apply to all mention labels, including general spelling rules.

    Positive Rules

    Rules specifying the entities that should be annotated:

    • Every mention to groups in the following categories: ethnicity, religion, sexual orientation, gender, disability, intersectional. These labels will start with the prefix G.
    • Every offensive mention to groups in the preceding categories. These labels will start with the prefix I.
    • Every offensive/insulting word.
    • Every attack intent in the following categories: dehumanization, derogation, animosity, threatening, pro-hate.
    • There can be more than one entity in a single sentence.
    • There can be annotations inside annotations (e.g., a group mentioned inside an intent).
    • Words containing orthographic errors or typos should be annotated as well.

    Negative Rules

    Rules specifying the entities that should not be annotated:

    • Mentions of groups not considered in the preceding categories.
    • Negative connotative adjectives (e.g., "you are so lazy").
    • Words that are implicit attacks in context but not out of context (e.g., "parasite" used in "These dishes are full of parasites").

    Multi-word Rules

    Rules specifying whether a group of words should be annotated under a single label:

    • Both groups, attacks, and offensive mentions can be composed of multi-words (n-grams).

    Group Mentions

    Identify mentions of specific groups. Both direct and implied mentions should be annotated.

    The groups are as follows:

    Ethnicity

    Mentions of ethnic groups such as Latino or Black in a neutral way, focusing on the designation without introducing any negative implications.

    Example: "The community center serves a diverse population including Latinos and Blacks."

    Religion

    References to religions such as Judaism or Christianity that merely identify the group without derogatory context.

    Example: "The charity event was attended by Jewish and Christian community leaders."

    Gender

    Mentions of gender where the reference is purely descriptive and devoid of any judgment.

    Example: "The survey included participants of various genders, including male, female, and non-binary."

    Sexual Orientation

    Mentions of sexual orientation that state this characteristic without negative connotations.

    Example: "The support group welcomes members who identify as gay, straight, or bisexual."

    Disability

    References to disabilities in a factual manner, ensuring the mention is respectful and non-pejorative.

    Example: "Facilities have been updated to accommodate individuals with physical disabilities."

    Working Class

    Mentions of socio-economic groups classified under the term 'working class' in a neutral way.

    Example: "The union meeting was primarily attended by individuals from the working class, discussing their rights and future."

    Ideological Group

    References to political or ideological groups without portraying them in a derogatory context.

    Example: "The debate featured speakers from diverse ideological backgrounds, including communists, nazis, and fascists."

    Intersectional

    Instances where multiple group identities are mentioned together in a non-offensive manner.

    Example: "The workshop highlighted issues faced by Black women in tech industries."

    Offensive Denominations

    Annotate any slurs, derogatory terms, or offensive names used to describe individuals or groups.

    Ethnicity

    Terms that derogate ethnic backgrounds through negative stereotypes or slurs.

    Example: "The term nigger is an offensive denomination towards Black people."

    Religion

    Terms used pejoratively to describe religious affiliations.

    Example: "Using zealot pejoratively can derogate an individual's religious fervor."

    Gender

    Derogatory terms that malign or stereotype gender identities.

    Example: "Calling a woman hysterical woman often carries sexist connotations"

    Sexual Orientation

    Slurs that negatively target individuals based on their sexual orientation.

    Example: "The slur dyke has been historically used to insult lesbian women."

    Disability

    Terms that derogatively refer to someone's physical or mental disabilities.

    Example: "Using cripple as an insult is highly disrespectful and offensive."

    Working Class

    Derogatory terms that demean socioeconomic status or class background.

    Example: "Calling someone trailer trash is an offensive denomination that stereotypes individuals from lower economic classes."

    Ideological Group

    Terms used pejoratively to describe political or ideological groups.

    Example: "Terms like commie for communists or fascist used inappropriately can derogate individuals based on their political beliefs."

    Intersectional

    Derogatory terms that target multiple identity factors simultaneously.

    Example: "Using welfare queen can be an offensive denomination that combines stereotypes of race, gender, and economic status."

    Hate Speech Intent

    Evaluate the hate speech intent behind the statement:

    Dehumanization or Comparison

    Content that likens individuals or groups to non-human entities, thereby stripping them of humanity.

    Example: "Immigrants are parasites."

    Threatening Language

    Statements that threaten or imply violence towards specific groups.

    Example: "Women should be shot. Agree!."

    Derogation

    Explicit language that demeans, demonizes, or insults groups based on inherent traits.

    Example: "Truly, I have been to Poland and there is nothing to see there. Polish people are so nasty as well"

    Animosity

    Subtle or covert expressions of hostility that may not be overtly aggressive but still convey prejudice.

    Example: "I don't want to be close to a breastfeeding woman."

    Hate Crime

    Endorsements or justifications of hate crimes and hateful ideologies.

    Example: " Hitler was right all along. We are witnessing this at home everyday."

    Offensive Words

    Tag offensive words, including insults, threats, or calls for violence.

    • "You're such an idiot"

    • "That guy is a complete moron."

    • "Go back to where you came from, slur."

    • "You're nothing but a loser."

    Examples of Hate Speech Annotations

    Explore various examples of hate speech annotations and how they can be tagged based on different categories.

    Example 1 - Offensive Denominations

    Sentence: "You're such a moron!
    Annotation: The word "moron" is tagged as an offensive word.

    Example 2 - Hate Speech Intent

    Sentence: "All immigrants should be deported."
    Annotation: The phrase "All immigrants" is tagged under hate speech intent for "dehumanization."

    Example 3 - Group Mentions

    Sentence: "The support group is open to gay, straight, and bisexual individuals."
    Annotation: The words "gay," "straight," and "bisexual" are tagged as group mentions.
