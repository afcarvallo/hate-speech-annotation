Hate Speech Annotation Guidelines

Introduction

Hate speech encompasses any form of expression that can incite, promote, or justify hatred, violence, or discrimination against a person or group based on race, religion, ethnicity, gender, disability, working class, ideology or sexual orientation. It can manifest explicitly through clear, direct language, such as racial slurs or calls for violence against a group ("All [group] should be expelled from the country"). Conversely, it can appear implicitly through more subtle means, such as coded language or stereotypes that indirectly suggest negativity ("Those people are always causing trouble," where "those people" refers to a specific ethnic or racial group).

This project aims to create the first token-level annotated corpus identifying instances of hate speech focused on specific groups, along with the detection of explicit and implicit expressions of hate. This annotation guide is designed to support annotators and will utilize INCEpTION software, which is standard in the field. The dataset generated from this guide will be instrumental in enhancing algorithms for detecting hate speech, extending beyond traditional detection methods by also annotating group mentions, offensive denominations, offensive words and the intent behind the hate speech. This token-level annotation approach provides deeper insights into the context and specific targets of hate speech, thus improving the precision of detection systems. Additionally, the dataset will increase transparency regarding the criteria used by automated systems to identify hate speech, helping users understand the detailed decision-making process behind content moderation. A key component of this project involves a pre-annotation process where data output from a Language Model (LLM) is initially reviewed to ensure a diverse representation of group mentions, offensive denominations, types of attacks, and non-hate content. To ensure the highest quality of annotation, we will implement a rigorous pre-annotation process coupled with a training regimen that utilizes the flashcard spaced repetition method. This method is specifically designed to ensure that annotators thoroughly learn and understand the task requirements. By reinforcing knowledge consistently over time, it aims to boost retention and proficiency. The process will also assess the inter-agreement among annotators, requiring that it exceed a specified threshold. Annotators whose performance falls below this threshold will not be included in the main annotation tasks, as their lower inter-agreement rates may indicate a performance that does not meet our projectâ€™s standards for accuracy and consistency. By addressing the data post-LLM processing and rigorously screening annotator consistency, we aim to correct and balance the dataset effectively. This step seeks to avoid perpetuating any biases that might be present in the initial LLM outputs.
The fundamental premise governing all annotation rules in this protocol is to annotate the shortest and most general expression possible, ensuring that the entity is fully described, even if it requires an understanding of the context that spans over more than one of the preceding sentences.
